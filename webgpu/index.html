Reservoir Cascades

Radiance Cascades compute global illumination on a principled multi-scale grid, but their deterministic interpolation merge leaks light, rings, and destroys small bright sources — errors that never average away. This demo replaces that merge with ReSTIR-style resampling, converting the structural bias into noise that time averages out. Flip the mode switch to compare.

What am I looking at?

Each frame traces one ray per probe-bin into a 2D SDF scene and merges radiance top-down through a 4–5 level cascade. In vanilla mode the merge is the standard deterministic bilinear interpolation: on S2 it shatters into the ringing “snowflake” and a blobbed light fan; on S1 it leaks light behind the thin bar. In ours the merge is resampled importance sampling over the same cascade — grain instead of structure, and the grain is unbiased. ρ is the validation-ray probability (kills residual leaks); M₀ the temporal confidence cap; live mask-energy metrics print below the canvas and match the paper’s CPU-oracle numbers.

Try this: on S2, drag ρ to 0. A faint arc of ghost light appears in the shadow — pixels whose sightline crosses the wall exactly at a cascade interval boundary, where coarse-level parents’ rays start beyond the wall and never test it. That arc is the paper’s interval-boundary leak (Lemma C) made visible; raising ρ re-tests those reconnections and extinguishes it.
